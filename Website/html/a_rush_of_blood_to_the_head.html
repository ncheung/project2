Ten years later, still a seamless album

I've been listening to this album for ten years. Ten years. That's an amazing amount of time. Well, not that amazing. There are people who have been listening to Zep II for forty-two years. But I digress. This album has stood the test of time. It was a welcome repository for my teenage angst when it dropped in 2003, and compelled me to purchase a Death Cab for Cutie patch for my patched sweatshirt (remember those?). Now, as a grown up, this record brings out a different response; nostalgia, contemplation, reflection. And it makes my toes tap. Especially on The Sound of Settling. The album flows seamlessly, each track blending into the next, and is designed for playing on repeat, as the last song bleeds into the first. If you like bare-bones indie rock, with some ballads thrown in the mix, you'll love this record. In fact, if you're even considering buying this, just buy it. Okay? (This review is for the vinyl version, which I purchased from seller marvelio. The album was new and in original shrink wrap. There was some slight tearing on the album cover before opening (due to inadequate packaging protection), but that seems to be the norm with third-party sellers. The double LPs were unscratched and unwarped, and play well on my deck.)

- Brandon Flowers

Great album!

Released at around the same time bands like Jimmy Eat World were recieving critical and commercial success, Death Cab For Cutie's 2003 album Transatlanticism is a defining moment in its era of music, taking the so called emo genre to new and complex heights while still keeping its youthful appeal. A unique collection of songs that are observant yet emotional, this record still ramains their most inspired moment in my opinion. The group would change direction completely on their major label debut, Plans, that would follow this and some would say more ambitious areas but there is something subtle in this work that makes it a masterpiece.

- Billie Jean

Taking This Volume Back Off the Shelf

The first time I'd ever heard Death Cab For Cutie (DCFC) I was working in a movie theatre during the summer of 2005; 'Sound of Settling' had been attached to the trailer for Shopgirl and would play 2-3 times on the television above the cash register. The combination of Chris Walla's voice and rippling images of vulnerability absolutely captivated me. I would stop whatever I was doing at the 'Sound of Settling' to embrace one brief moment of clarity and sophistication in an otherwise tumultuous work environment. DCFC's songs are so powerful that you will remember your first experience as potently as I did. 'Transatlanticism' floods you with intensity, infusing you with melodic tunes and heartfelt lyrics; the halo of this album is an eclectic array of 'brokenhearted lullabies' and 'youthful anthems of optimism'. The nearly eight minute long title song 'Transatlanticism' cannot sum the entirity of this album any more perfectly in its rallying cry: "I need you so much closer". DCFC has carried me through so much in the years since I've discovered them. As other fans agree, their melodies/lyrics hold a maturity that will carry you through good, bad, and in between. It will grow with you and provide the musical nourishment to cultivate a well tempered soul.

- Alan Bright
